# HLS OPIA Legal Resume Format Guide

This document encodes the resume formatting and content rules published by the
Harvard Law School Bernard Koteen Office of Public Interest Advising (OPIA) in
their Resume Layout Guidance. These rules represent widely-adopted best
practices for legal resumes and are applicable to any law student or aspiring
law student, not only those at Harvard Law School.

Source: HLS OPIA Job Search Toolkit -- Resume Layout Guidance
(https://hls.harvard.edu/bernard-koteen-office-of-public-interest-advising/opia-job-search-toolkit/resume-layout-guidance/)

---

## Header

- Include your name, current address, phone number, and professional email
  address.
- If you are a current student, use your law school email address.
- You may include a permanent address in addition to your school address to
  signal geographic ties.
- You may include personal pronouns after your name or in the address line.
  Examples: "Alex Washington (she/her/hers)" or "they/them" after the address.
- **Do not** include a job objective on the resume. Career objectives belong in
  the cover letter.
- **Do not** include a photo or headshot. Use that space for content about your
  experiences and accomplishments.

## Education

- Unless you are more than approximately five years out of law school, the
  Education section should precede the Experience section.
- For current students, indicate the degree you expect to earn and your
  anticipated graduation date. Examples: "J.D., May 2026"; "LL.M., May 2025."
- List all advanced degrees in reverse chronological order, starting with your
  law degree or most recent degree and working back to college.
- Reflect transfers, study abroad, and joint degree programs in this section.
- Latin honors (*magna*, *summa*, *cum laude*) must appear in italicized,
  lowercase letters. Example: "B.S. *magna cum laude* in Physics."
- Consider including a "Relevant Coursework" subsection under your law school
  entry to demonstrate interest in a particular area, especially when
  substantive experience is limited.

## Honors and Activities

- Include two separate subsections for each educational degree: "Honors" and
  "Activities."
- Include at most three to five entries per subsection. Listing too many
  dilutes the most relevant ones.
- Provide a brief explanation of any awards or distinctions that are not
  widely familiar.
- The Honors subsection should list notable awards or distinctions: Dean's
  List, Phi Beta Kappa, etc. Note that Dean's List may seem redundant if you
  also graduated with Latin honors.
- The Activities subsection should list relevant or personally meaningful
  student organizations, reading groups, elected offices, or sports.
- **Do not** include your undergraduate GPA unless it is specifically requested
  by an employer or not well-reflected in honors received.
- **Do not** include LSAT scores or other standardized test scores. Public
  service employers are rarely interested in test performance.

## Thesis and Publications

- If your thesis topic is relevant to your career interests, include a
  separate "Thesis" subsection under the appropriate educational degree with
  the title in italics.
- Notes or articles written for a journal or other publication should go under
  a separate "Publications" section in Bluebook citation format.
- Include notations such as "(publication pending)" or "(forthcoming, spring
  2026)" where appropriate.

## Experience

- List work, internship, and significant volunteer experiences in reverse
  chronological order, with the most recent first.
- Clinical work, internships, part-time work, and volunteer work are all valid
  experience entries. Volunteer work need not be separated under a distinct
  heading unless you have many entries and want to break up sections.
- **Do not** feel compelled to list every job. Design the resume to highlight
  the most significant and relevant experiences.
- Be mindful of chronological gaps. If excluding a job creates a significant
  unexplained gap, consider including it or finding creative ways to address
  the gap.
- Include all work experience, including service industry, retail, hospitality,
  and child care. Research shows service industry experience is a strong
  predictor of professional success.

### Writing Experience Descriptions

- Paint a dynamic picture of responsibilities and accomplishments without
  exaggeration. Add detail: types of clients, types of cases, scope of work.
- Use longer descriptions for experiences most critical to your current job
  search. Employers assume that description length reflects how much priority
  you give to the experience.
- Break descriptions into shorter phrases that are easily scanned, not full
  sentences.
- **Start each bullet with an action verb.** Examples: "Drafted Congressional
  testimony." "Launched summer intern program." "Advised 50 first-year
  students on course selection."
- **Eliminate passive verbs**, especially any form of "to be." Change "Was
  responsible for running" to "Ran."
- If you held multiple roles at the same employer, list both under one header
  with each title in italics.

## Dates

- Place dates flush with the right-hand margin of the page.
- Refer to summer or semester-long jobs as "Summer 20__," "Fall 20__,"
  "Spring 20__."
- In most cases, specifying exact months is unnecessary. Use year ranges:
  "2019 -- 2021."
- Use a consistent dash style throughout (either en dash or hyphen, but not
  both).

## Skills and Interests

- If you have language skills beyond English, note them honestly. Do not
  overstate or understate your fluency. You may be asked to demonstrate
  language skills in an interview.
- Adding a line about outside hobbies or interests is valuable for interviews,
  giving the interviewer additional conversation topics. Be prepared to discuss
  anything listed here.
- **Do not** list basic technology skills (Microsoft Office, LexisNexis,
  Westlaw). These do not distinguish you from other law student candidates.
- **Do** list specialized technical proficiencies that may be relevant: GIS
  mapping, coding languages, social media analytics, data analysis tools, etc.

## References

- **Do not** add "References will be furnished upon request" to your resume.
  Employers ask for references separately, and this line wastes space.
- A reference sheet should use the same header as your resume. Include three
  to four references with: name, title, email, organization name, address,
  phone number, and a line about how each reference knows you.
- Choose references strategically for different employers.
- Always ask permission before listing someone as a reference. Inform them
  about the type of work you are pursuing.

## Formatting and Consistency

- Keep font color black. Not red, blue, or orange. Subtle accent color is
  acceptable but discouraged.
- Maintain consistent formatting throughout: same indentation, same bullet
  style (do not mix bullets and hyphens), same dash style for date ranges,
  same capitalization conventions.
- Target one page for students with less experience; two pages maximum for
  students with extensive pre-law careers.
- Proofread multiple times for consistency in font, indentation, bullet style,
  dash style (hyphen vs. en dash), and capitalization.

## Common Mistakes to Flag

These are the most frequently seen violations of the above rules:

1. Including a photo or headshot
2. Including an LSAT score or standardized test score
3. Including a job objective on the resume
4. Writing "References available upon request"
5. Using passive voice ("Was responsible for...") instead of action verbs
6. Mixing bullet styles (bullets in one section, hyphens in another)
7. Inconsistent date formatting (months in some entries, seasons in others)
8. Listing Microsoft Office or Westlaw under skills
9. Including high school achievements when the student has sufficient
   college and law school content to fill the page
10. Exceeding two pages
